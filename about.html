About

I am a scientist and phenomenologist exploring the nature of observation and understanding in biological research. Currently at Lund University, I study how human expertise, biological complexity, and technological capabilities come together in microscopy research.

Through my domain-driven approach to microscopy, I investigate the delicate interplay between three forms of intelligence: the researcher's intuitive understanding, the inherent patterns of cellular behavior, and the analytical power of microscope systems. This perspective helps bridge the gap between raw microscopy images and meaningful biological insights.

My work spans scientific research, philosophical inquiry, and poetic expression – each offering unique ways to explore and understand the phenomena we observe in life sciences.

Current Focus

I'm developing frameworks that acknowledge and harmonize three key domains in microscopy research: human cognition, biological self-organization, and microscope automation. This approach aims to transform how we extract insights from microscopic observations.

By treating each domain as a distinct yet interconnected system of intelligence, we can better understand both the phenomena we observe and the process of observation itself.

Goals

  • To bridge the gap between microscopy images and biological insight through a domain-driven understanding of human expertise, cellular behavior, and microscope capabilities.
  • To develop frameworks that honor both the objective rigor of scientific measurement and the subjective experience of discovery.
  • To explore the nature of observation itself, understanding how different forms of intelligence - human, biological, and technological - contribute to scientific knowledge.
